  • Neuron:

    Cells in the nervous system that act as interconnected information processors, which are essential for all of the tasks of the nervous system

  • Myelin Sheath:

    Fatty substance that insulates axons

  • Peripheral Nervous System (PNS):

    Connects the brain and spinal cord to the muscles, organs and senses in the periphery of the body

  • Endocrine System:

    Series of glands that produce chemical substances known as hormones

  • Scientific Method:

    Theory (well-developed set of ideas that propose an explanation for observed phenomena) => Hypothesis (tentative and testable statement about the relationship between two or more variables) => Research => Observation

  • Naturalistic Observation:

    Observation of behavior in its natural setting

  • Case/Clinical Study:

    Observational research study focusing on one or a few people

  • Correlation:

    Relationship between two or more variables; when two variables are correlated, one variable changes as the other does

  • Independent Variable:

    Variable that is influenced or controlled by the experimenter; in a sound experimental study, the independent variable is the only important difference between the experimental and control group

  • Dependent Variable:

    Variable that the researcher measures to see how much effect the independent variable had

  • Cocktail Party Effect:

    The cocktail party effect is the phenomenon of the brain's ability to focus one's auditory attention on a particular stimulus while filtering out a range of other stimuli

  • Pupil:

    Small opening in the eye through which light passes

  • Sensitization:

    Non-associative learning process through which repeated exposure to a stimulus results in the progressive amplification (increasing strength) of the reaction to the stimulus

  • Narcolepsy:

    Sleep disorder in which the sufferer cannot resist falling to sleep at inopportune times

  • Apnea:

    Sleep disorder defined by episodes during which breathing stops during sleep

  • Withdrawal:

    Variety of negative symptoms experienced when drug use is discontinued

  • Holophrase:

    A single word (such as OK) that is used to express a complete, meaningful thought.

  • Attachment:

    Long-standing connection or bond with others

  • Fluid Intelligence:

    Ability to see complex relationships and solve problems

  • Crystallized Intelligence:

    Characterized by acquired knowledge and the ability to retrieve it

  • Wernicke-Korsakoff Syndrome:

    Happens as a result of not getting enough vitamin B1. Wernicke includes confusion, loss of muscle coordination, and trouble with your vision. Korsakoff is slow and damages part of brain involved with memory

  • The Big Five:

    Most common personality traits - Openness to Experience, Conscientiousness, Extroversion, Agreeableness, Neuroticism. Each trait represent a spectrum between two polar ends (e.g. introverted vs extroverted)

  • Ectomorph:

    A person with a lean and delicate body build

  • Fixation:

    An obsessive interest in or feeling about someone or something

  • Schizotypal:

    A mental disorder characterized by severe social anxiety, thought disorder, paranoid ideation, derealization, transient psychosis and often unconventional belief

  • Paranoid Delusion:

    Characterized by beliefs that others are out to harm them

  • Borderline:

    Instability in interpersonal relationships, self-image, and mood, as well as impulsivity; key features include intolerance of being alone and fear of abandonment, unstable relationships, unpredictable behavior and moods, and intense and inappropriate anger

  • Halo Effect:

    The tendency for an impression created in one area to influence opinion in another area

  • Matching Hypothesis:

    A theory of interpersonal attraction which argues that relationships are formed between two people who are equal or very similar in terms of social desirability

  • Proximity:

    Things that are close to one another tend to be grouped together

  • Dendrite:

    Branch-like extension of the soma that receives incoming signals from other neurons

  • Central Nervous System (CNS):

    Brain and spinal cord

  • Corpus Callosum:

    Thick band of neural fibers connecting the brain’s two hemispheres

  • Broca’s Area:

    Region in the left hemisphere that is essential for language production

  • Validity:

    Accuracy of a given result in measuring what it is designed to measure

  • Random Sample:

    Subset of a larger population in which every member of the population has an equal chance of being selected

  • Wording Effects:

    The possible effects on participants caused by the order of presented words or even the choice of the words themselves

  • Experiment:

    A scientific procedure undertaken to make a discovery, test a hypothesis, or demonstrate a known fact

  • Experimental Group:

    Group designed to answer the research question; experimental manipulation is the only difference between the experimental and control groups, so any differences between the two are due to experimental manipulation rather than chance

  • Control Group:

    serves as a basis for comparison and controls for chance factors that might influence the results of the study—by holding such factors constant across groups so that the experimental manipulation is the only difference between groups

  • Placebo Effect:

    People's expectations or beliefs influencing or determining their experience in a given situation

  • Threshold of Excitation:

    Level of charge in the membrane that causes the neuron to become active

  • Iris:

    Colored portion of the eye

  • Accommodation:

    Adjustment of a schema by changing a scheme to accommodate new information different from what was already known

  • Night Terrors:

    Sleep disorder in which the sleeper experiences a sense of panic and may scream or attempt to escape from the immediate environment

  • Somnambulism:

    Sleepwalking

  • Tolerance:

    State of requiring increasing quantities of the drug to gain the desired effect

  • Amotivational Syndrome:

    A lack of desire to complete tasks, a sense of apathy about the future, poor concentration, and decreased interest in social and other activities

  • Telegraphic Speech:

    A form of communication consisting of simple two-word long sentences often composed of a noun and a verb that adhere to the grammatical standards of the culture's language

  • Introversion:

    The tendency to be concerned with one's own thoughts and feelings rather than with external things

  • Mesomorph:

    A person with a compact and muscular body build

  • Schizoid:

    A personality disorder characterized by a lack of interest in social relationships, a tendency towards a solitary or sheltered lifestyle, secretiveness, emotional coldness, detachment and apathy

  • DID/MPD:

    Dissociative disorder (formerly known as multiple personality disorder) in which a person exhibits two or more distinct, well-defined personalities or identities and experiences memory gaps for the time during which another identity emerged

  • Narcissism:

    Personality disorder characterized by a long-term pattern of exaggerated feelings of self-importance, an excessive need for admiration, and a lack of empathy toward other people

  • Munchausen By Proxy:

    A mental health problem in which a caregiver makes up or causes an illness or injury in a person under his or her care, such as a child, an elderly adult, or a person who has a disability

  • Mere Exposure Effect:

    A psychological phenomenon by which people tend to develop a preference for things merely because they are familiar with them

  • Social Loafing:

    Exertion of less effort by a person working in a group because individual performance cannot be evaluated separately from the group, thus causing performance decline on easy tasks
